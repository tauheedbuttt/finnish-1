## Verb Types (1–5)

Finnish verbs are grouped into types. Each type has its own rule for removing endings and conjugating.

### Verb Type 1
- Ends in **2 vowels** (e.g., aa, ua, oa)
- Remove the **last 1 letter**
- Add personal endings
- Applies **KPT Type A** (strong → weak in personal forms)
- Example: *puhua* → puhu- → puhun (I speak)

### Verb Type 2
- Ends with **da/dä**
- Remove the **last 2 letters**
- Add personal endings
- **No KPT change**
- Example: *syödä* → syö- → syön (I eat)

### Verb Type 3
- Ends with **la, na, sta, ra** (remember: la-na-sta-ra)
- Remove the **last 2 letters**
- Add **"e"** + personal endings
- Applies **KPT Type B** (weak in basic form → strong in personal forms)
- Example: *opiskella* → opiskel- → opiskelen (I study)

### Verb Type 4
- Ends with **ata, uta, ota** (or their front-vowel equivalents)
- Remove the **last 2 letters**
- Add **"a/ä"** + personal ending
- Applies **KPT Type B**
- Example: *haluta* → halu- → haluan (I want)

### Verb Type 5
- Ends with **ita/itä**
- Remove the **last 1 letter**
- Add **"se"** + personal endings
- **No KPT change**
- Example: *tarvita* → tarvit- → tarvitsen (I need)

### Personal Endings (all verb types)

| Pronoun | Ending |
|---------|--------|
| mä | -n |
| sä | -t |
| hän/se | — (no ending, stem only) |
| me | -mme / -taan (spoken passive) |
| te | -tte |
| he/ne | -vat/-vät |

## KPT Change

KPT refers to the consonant letters **K, P, T**. These letters change form depending on the case ending that follows them.

### What changes

| Strong | Weak |
|--------|------|
| kk | k |
| pp | p |
| tt | t |
| k | — (disappears) |
| p | v |
| t | d |
| nk | ng |
| nt | nn |
| lt | ll |
| rt | rr |
| mp | mm |

### When does KPT change happen?

The ending after the consonant must be:
- **CC + V** (consonant cluster + vowel), OR
- **C** (single consonant)

### When does KPT NOT change?

- Ending is **V + N**, **V**, or **VV** (vowel patterns)
- **S + K/P/T** — e.g., *lasku*
- Mixed KPT — e.g., *kp, kt, pk*
- **Loanwords** — e.g., *auto, shampoo*
- **Person's names** (unless they have kk/pp/tt)

### Where is KPT?

KPT change only ever happens in the **last syllable** of the word stem.

## Past Tense — Chapter 1 Intro

The basic rule: add **"i"** before the personal ending to form the past tense.

### Normal verb conjugation (past)

| | OLLA (to be) | KÄYDÄ (to visit + missä) | TULLA (to come) |
|--|--|--|--|
| mä | olin | kävin | tulin |
| sä | olit | kävit | tulit |
| se | oli | kävi | tuli |
| me | oltiin* | kävitiin* | tulitiin* |
| te | olitte | kävitte | tulitte |
| ne | oli | kävi | tuli |

*Spoken Finnish uses the passive form for "me".

### "I have" (Mulla/Sulla) in past

For the "have" structure, change *on* to *oli*:
- *Mulla oli* (I had)
- *Sulla oli* (You had)
- *Hänellä oli* (He/She had)

Example: *Mä kävin Turussa* (I visited Turku)

## Local Cases — Missä

The **missä** case answers the question "where?" for location. It is used with **static verbs** — verbs without movement (olla, asua, istua).

The ending is either **-ssa/-ssä** or **-lla/-llä**.

### S-case (-ssa/-ssä) — used for:
- Closed places (inside a building)
- Inside a location
- Place names
- Areas (park, forest, tori)

### L-case (-lla/-llä) — used for:
- Outside
- On a surface
- Near a place

### Special words
- *kotona* (at home) — does not use -ssa or -lla
- *ulkona* (outside) — does not use -ssa or -lla

## Word Types (e and nen)

Nouns and adjectives are divided into word types based on their basic form, stem, and partitive singular form.

### Words ending in "e"
- Stem: e → **ee**
- Examples: *Tampere* → Tamperee-, *huone* → huonee-
- Partitive: e → e + **"tta"** (e.g., *huonetta*)

### Words ending in "nen"
- Stem: nen → **se**
- Examples: *ihminen* → ihmise-, *iloinen* → iloise-
- Partitive: drop nen, add **"sta"** (e.g., *ihmistä*)

### Possessive suffixes
Used in formal/written Finnish when a genitive pronoun precedes the noun.

| Spoken | Written suffix |
|--------|---------------|
| mun ystävä | ystävä**ni** |
| sun ystävä | ystävä**si** |
| hänen ystävä | ystävä**nsä** |
| meidän ystävä | ystävä**mme** |
| teidän ystävä | ystävä**nne** |
| heidän ystävä | ystävä**nsä** |

In spoken Finnish, just use *mun kirja*, *sun auto* etc.

## Sentence Types: Possessive

Used to express **ownership or having something**.

### Structure
**Owner + -lla/-llä + on + owned thing**

- *Mulla on kirja.* (I have a book.)
- *Ashokilla on auto.* (Ashok has a car.)
- Any noun or name: add **-lla/-llä** to it

### Rules
- The adjective describing the owned thing also takes **-lla/-llä**
- Negative: owned thing goes into **partitive** form
  - *Mulla ei ole kirjaa.* (I don't have a book.)

### Expressing feelings (same structure)
- *Mulla on nälkä.* (I am hungry. Literally: I have hunger.)
- *Mulla on kylmä.* (I am cold.)
- **Negative for feelings does NOT use partitive:**
  - *Mulla ei ole nälkä.* (I am not hungry.)

## Diphthongs

A **diphthong** is two vowels together in **one syllable**, pronounced as a single sound.

Examples: *ai, ei, oi, ui, yi, äi, öi, au, eu, iu, ou, äy, öy, ey, iy*

Key rule: the two vowels are not separated across syllables — they stay together.

---

## Word Types (i)

Some words ending in **"i"** do not follow standard rules. These are usually:
- Modern words
- Compound words
- Loanwords
- Multi-syllable words

### Type 1 — ends in hi, li, ri, ni (hilirini)
- Stem: i → **e**
- Example: *suuri* → suure-, *pieni* → piene-
- Partitive: i → **"ta"** (e.g., *suurta*)
- **No KPT change**

### Type 2 — ends in ki, mi, pi, ti, vi (kimipitivi)
- Stem: i → **e**
- Example: *talvi* → talve-
- Partitive: i → **"ea"** (e.g., *talvea*)
- **Does KPT change**

### Type 3 — ends in "si"
- Stem: si → **de**
- Example: *vesi* → vede-
- Partitive: si → **"ttä"** (e.g., *vettä*)
- **Does KPT change**
- Important words: *vuosi* (year), *kuukausi* (month), *uusi* (new), *käsi* (hand), *viisi* (5), *kuusi* (6)

## Sentence Types: Existential Sentence

Used to express that **something exists somewhere**.

### Structure (top to bottom)

1. **PLACE + -ssa/-lla** (where it exists)
2. **ON** (is) or **EI OO** (is not)
3. **OBJECT** — the thing that exists, in partitive or nominative form

Example:
- *Huoneessa on sohva.* (There is a sofa in the room.)
- *Pöydällä ei ole kirjaa.* (There is no book on the table.)

The sentence asks: **in where, is what?**

## Postpositions

Postpositions describe the **location of one thing relative to another**. Unlike English prepositions (before the noun), Finnish postpositions come **after** the noun. The noun before a postposition is in the **genitive** form.

Example: *koira on laatikon edessä* (the dog is in front of the box)

## Imperative

The imperative is the verb form used to **give orders, instructions, warnings, wishes, or recipes**.

### How to form the positive imperative
1. Take the basic form of the verb
2. Get the first person (mä form): e.g., *nukkua* → *nukun*
3. Remove the **"n"**: *nukku!* (Sleep!)

### How to form the negative imperative
Same as positive, then add **älä** at the start:
- *älä nukku!* (Don't sleep!)

### Object rules
- Positive imperative: object is in **nominative**
- Negative imperative: object is in **partitive**
- Exception: if there's another reason for partitive, it still wins

### Polite forms
- *ole hyvä* — please (literally: be good)
- *ole kiltti* — be kind / please

### Usage
- Warnings
- General signs
- Instructions / recipes
- Wishes

## Local Cases — Mistä, Mihin

These answer "from where?" and "to where?"

### Mistä — from where?

| Type | Ending | Used when |
|------|--------|-----------|
| Internal | **-sta/-stä** | coming from inside a place |
| External | **-lta/-ltä** | coming from outside of a place |

Attach to the **stem** of the word.

### Mihin — to where?

#### Internal endings (going into a place)

| Pattern | Rule | Examples |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Vn** | Stem ends in 1 vowel → add "n" | talo → taloon, Suomi → Suomeen |
| | If word ends in si/ia/ea → double the vowel + n | uusi → uuteen, Italia → Italiaan |
| | Long words → add "en" | |
| **seen** | Stem ends in 2 identical vowels | huone → huoneeseen, Lontoo → Lontooseen |
| **hVn** | Single syllable word ending in 2 vowels | maa → maahan, suu → suuhun, puu → puuhun |
| | Weekdays ending in -tai → add -hin | maanantai → maanantaihin |

#### External ending (going to a surface/near a place)
- Add **-lle** to the stem

#### Summary

| # | Pattern | Examples |
|---|---------|---------|
| 1 | Vn — stem ending in one vowel | koulu → kouluun, Suomi → Suomeen |
| 2 | seen — longer word ending in two identical vowels | huone → huoneeseen |
| 3 | hVn — short word ending in two vowels | puu → puuhun, maa → maahan |

**Note:** Local cases are also used in certain expressions that are not literally about place.

## Intonation

### What is Intonation?

**Intonation** is the "melody" or pitch pattern of your voice when you speak — the ups and downs as you talk. Every language has its own unique intonation pattern.

### Finnish Intonation Basics

- **Falling intonation is normal**: Most Finnish sentences fall at the end
- **More monotone than many languages**: Finnish is less musical than English or Spanish
- **Grammar does the work**: Finnish uses grammar endings and question words to show meaning, NOT pitch changes
  - Example: "You study Finnish" (statement) vs "You study Finnish?" (question)
  - In English: question goes UP at the end
  - In Finnish: BOTH fall — use **-ko/-kö** endings or question words (mikä, missä) instead

### Two Key Intonation Patterns

#### 1. Terminal Intonation (Falling Pitch)
- Pitch **drops at the end** of a phrase
- Signals: "I'm finished, it's your turn to speak"
- Example: *Joo, mennään vaan* (Yeah, let's go)
- Common in statements and ending conversation turns

#### 2. Continuation Intonation (Flat or Rising)
- Pitch **stays mid-level or rises slightly**
- Signals: "I'm not done yet, I have more to say"
- Common in stories, explanations, and lists
- Prevents awkward pauses or interruptions

### Questions in Finnish

#### Questions usually fall (like statements)
- *Mennäänkö kahville?* (Should we go for coffee?) — falls at the end
- You know it's a question from grammar clues:
  - The **-ko/-kö** ending
  - Question words: *mikä, missä, milloin, kuka*

#### Sometimes questions rise (informal)
- A single word with rising tone becomes a question
- Example: *Kuitti?* (Receipt?) — said by a cashier with rising pitch

### Why Learn Intonation?

1. Better listening — recognise when Finns are asking questions or continuing
2. Better speaking — listeners understand you more easily
3. Better pronunciation — intonation and pronunciation improve together

### How to Practice

1. Listen and repeat longer phrases, not single words
2. Record yourself and compare to native speakers
3. Listen for places where your pitch goes up when it should stay flat
4. Practice regularly — small sessions beat cramming

### Key Takeaway

Finnish intonation is about **sentence endings** (falling vs. flat/rising), not dramatic pitch shifts.
- **Falling at end** = statement finished, your turn
- **Flat or rising** = I'm still talking
- **Grammar endings** tell you if it's a question, not the pitch

## Positive Imperfect Tense

### What is the Imperfect Tense?

The **imperfect tense** (imperfekti) talks about things that **already happened** in the past. It is the storytelling tense.

### When to Use Imperfect

- Completed past actions: *Eilen söin pizzaa* (Yesterday I ate pizza)
- Finished events — the event is over and done
- Often with time expressions: *eilen* (yesterday), *viime kesänä* (last summer), *vuonna 2001* (in 2001), *lapsena* (as a child)

### How to Form the Imperfect

**Basic formula: Verb stem + "i" + Personal ending**

- *nuku* (nukkua) + i + n = **nukuin** (I slept)
- *katso* (katsoa) + i + n = **katsoin** (I watched)
- *puhu* (puhua) + i + n = **puhuin** (I spoke)

**3rd person singular has NO personal ending:**
- *Hän nukui* (she slept) — no "n" at the end

### Vowel Changes in Imperfect

| Rule | What happens | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Back vowels (O, Ö, U, Y) | Stay the same | sanon → sanoin |
| Front vowels (A, Ä, E, I) | Drop when you add "i" | odotan → odotin |
| One-syllable stems | Lose first vowel of diphthong | saan → sain, syön → söin |

**For diphthongs:** Drop the first vowel of the diphthong, then add "i":
- *vie* → ve + i + n = **vein**
- *juo* → jo + i + n = **join**
- *syö* → sö + i + n = **söin**

**Tip:** Learn verbs with both present and imperfect forms together: *asua, asun, asuin*

### Verb Type 4 Uses "si" Instead of "i"

- *haluta* → halusin (I wanted)
- *tietää* → tiesin (I knew)

Some Verb Type 1 verbs also use "si": *ymmärtää* → ymmärsin, *lentää* → lensin

### Spoken vs. Written Forms

- **Me-form** uses passive in spoken Finnish: *me puhuttiin* (we spoke) instead of *me puhuimme*
- **Ne-form** may sound like 3rd singular: *ne sanoi* instead of *ne sanoivat*

### Irregular Verbs to Memorise

- **olla** (to be): mä olin, sä olit, se oli, me oltiin, te olitte, ne oli
- **käydä** (to visit/go): mä kävin, sä kävit, se kävi, me käytiin, te kävitte, ne kävi

### Example Conversation

*Mitä sä teit viikonloppuna?* (What did you do over the weekend?)
- *Mä olin kotona* (I was home)
- *Mä siivosin ja pesin pyykkiä* (I cleaned and did laundry)
- *Mä imuroin* (I vacuumed)
- *Mä katoin Netflixiä* (I watched Netflix)
- *Illalla mä menin ulos kavereitten kanssa* (In the evening I went out with friends)
- *Me mentiin saaristobaariin* (We went to an island bar)

### Quick Summary

| | |
|-|-|
| What | Past tense for finished, completed actions |
| How | Stem + "i" + ending (or "si" for Type 4) |
| Vowel changes | Learn per verb — back vowels stay, front vowels drop |
| 3rd person | No personal ending — just stem + "i" |

## Means of Transport

In Finnish, you say you travel **by** a vehicle by adding **-lla/-llä** to the transport word (the adessive case). This does not indicate location here — it purely means "by means of."

### Vowel harmony rule
- Word contains a, o, u → use **-lla**
- Word contains ä, ö, y → use **-llä**

### Vocabulary

| English | Finnish | By... |
|---------|---------|-------|
| bus | bussi | bussilla |
| taxi | taksi | taksilla |
| metro / subway | metro | metrolla |
| ship / boat | laiva | laivalla |
| bicycle | pyörä | pyörällä |
| tram | ratikka | ratikalla |
| train | juna | junalla |
| airplane | lentokone | lentokoneella |
| car | auto | autolla |
| motorcycle | moottoripyörä | moottoripyörällä |
| on foot | — | kävellen (no -lla ending — different word form) |

### Example sentences

- *Mä meen bussilla yliopistolle.* — I go by bus to the university.
- *Mä meen taksilla kotiin.* — I go by taxi home.
- *Mä meen metrolla Pasilasta keskustaan.* — I go by metro from Pasila to the city centre.
- *Mä meen laivalla Turusta Tukholmaan.* — I go by ship from Turku to Stockholm.
- *Mä meen pyörällä töihin.* — I go by bike to work.
- *Mä meen junalla Helsinkiin.* — I go by train to Helsinki.
- *Mä meen lentokoneella Saksaan.* — I go by airplane to Germany.
- *Mä meen kävellen kauppaan.* — I go on foot to the store.

## Customer Service Phrases

### Phone Call

**How to structure a call:**

1. **Say your name first**
   - *Täs on [nimi].* — This is [name].
2. **State why you are calling**
   - *Soitan kysyäkseni…* — I'm calling to ask…
   - *Mä haluaisin…* — I would like…
   - *Haluaisin varata ajan…* — I'd like to book an appointment…
   - *Mun täytyy varata aika…* — I need to make an appointment…
3. **Try Finnish first**
   - *Mä opiskelen suomea ja haluaisin yrittää käydä keskustelun suomeksi.* — I'm studying Finnish and would like to try having the conversation in Finnish.
4. **Switch to English if needed**
   - *Voinko puhua englanniksi?* — Can I speak in English?

### At the Hotel

**What you say:**
- *Hei, mulla on varaus kahdeksi yöksi.* — Hello, I have a reservation for two nights.
- *Hei, mulla on varaus nimellä Salo.* — Hello, I have a reservation under the name Salo.
- *Iltaa, mulla pitäisi olla varaus yhdeksi yöksi.* — Good evening, I should have a reservation for one night.

**What the receptionist says:**
- *Hei ja tervetuloa!* — Hello and welcome!
- *Katsotaas…* — Let me check…
- *Joo, täältä löytyy varaus Salo-nimellä.* — Yes, we have a reservation under the name Salo.
- *Saisinko henkilöllisyystodistuksen?* — May I see your ID, please?
- *Kirjoittaisitko henkilötiedot tähän lomakkeeseen?* — Could you fill in your personal details on this form?

### At the Restaurant

**Asking for a table:**
- *Hei, olisiko teillä tilaa kahdelle?* — Hello, do you have a table for two?
- *Moi, mulla ei oo varausta, mut olisko teillä tilaa yhdelle?* — Hi, I don't have a reservation, but do you have a table for one?
- *Hei, meitä on neljä, löytyisikö meille pöytää?* — Hello, there are four of us, is there a table available for us?

**What staff say:**
- *Nyt just on täyttä, mutta tuolta vapautuu kohta yksi pöytä.* — It's fully booked right now, but a table will be free soon.
- *Jäättekö odottamaan?* — Will you wait?
- *Meillä vapautuisi neljän hengen pöytä kahdeksalta.* — We will have a table for four available at eight.
- *Joo, tuolla takana on pöytä vapaana.* — Yes, there is a free table in the back.
- *Joo, valitkaa vaan mieleinen pöytä.* — Yes, just choose any table you like.

## Expressing Opinion

### Method 1: Using "musta" and the -sta/-stä Suffix

The **-sta/-stä** suffix (elative case) means "from" normally, but when attached to pronouns it means **"in [person]'s opinion"**.

| Pronoun | Opinion form |
|---------|-------------|
| mä | musta |
| sä | susta |
| hän/se | siitä |
| me | meistä |
| te | teistä |
| he/ne | heistä / niistä |

**Important:** Add directly to the pronoun stem (not mä or minä directly).

**With names:** Add linking vowel **-i** before -sta if name ends in a consonant:
- *Liisasta kahvi on hyvää* (In Lisa's opinion, coffee is good)

**Watch out:** *Musta* also means **black** in Finnish. Context makes it clear:
- *Musta auto* = black car
- *Musta kahvi on hyvää* = in my opinion, coffee is good

### Method 2: Using "mielestä"

**Formula: Genitive + mielestä**

| Spoken | Mielestä form |
|--------|--------------|
| mä | mun mielestä |
| sä | sun mielestä |
| hän | hänen mielestä |
| me | meidän mielestä |
| te | teidän mielestä |
| he/ne | heidän / niiden mielestä |

Both methods mean the same thing — use whichever feels natural.

### Asking for an Opinion

- *Mitä mieltä sinä olet [something]-sta/-stä?* — What do you think about...?
- *Onko [something] sinun mielestä [adjective]?* — Is [something] in your opinion [adjective]?

### Tykkää and Pitää (To Like)

Both mean "to like" and always require **-sta/-stä** on the thing you like:
- *Mä tykkään kahvista* (I like coffee)
- *Mä pidän suklaasta* (I like chocolate)
- Ask: *Mistä sinä tykkäät?* (What do you like?)

### Favorite Things — Lempi-

**Lempi** = favorite (as the first part of a compound word):
- *lempiruoka* = favorite food
- *lempijuoma* = favorite drink
- *lempi-ihminen* = favorite person

Example: *Mun lempiruoka on lasagne* (My favorite food is lasagne)

## Ordinal Numbers

### What are ordinal numbers?

Ordinal numbers tell you the **order or position** of something — 1st, 2nd, 3rd. In Finnish they are also used to **say and write dates**.

### The basic rule

Add **-s** to the end of a counting number:
- *neljä* (four) → *neljäs* (fourth)
- *seitsemän* (seven) → *seitsemäs* (seventh)

### Exceptions

| Situation | Rule | Examples |
|-----------|------|---------|
| Ends in **-si** | Drop the *i* before adding -s | *viisi* → *viides*, *kuusi* → *kuudes* |
| Ends in **-n** | Drop the *n* before adding -s | *kahdeksan* → *kahdeksas*, *yhdeksän* → *yhdeksäs* |
| *kolme* | Changes completely | *kolmas* |
| 1st | Completely different word | *ensimmäinen* (spoken: *eka*) |
| 2nd | Completely different word | *toinen* (spoken: *toka*) |

### Key ordinals (1–10)

| Number | Standard Finnish | Spoken Finnish |
|--------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1st | ensimmäinen | eka |
| 2nd | toinen | toka |
| 3rd | kolmas | kolmas |
| 4th | neljäs | neljäs |
| 5th | viides | viides |
| 6th | kuudes | kuues |
| 7th | seitsemäs | seitsemäs |
| 8th | kahdeksas | kaheksas |
| 9th | yhdeksäs | yheksäs |
| 10th | kymmenes | kymmenes |

### Dates in Finnish (Päivämäärät)

**Formula: Ordinal number + month name + -ta**

All Finnish months end in *-kuu*, so the partitive ending is always **-ta**.

| Date | Finnish |
|------|---------|
| 1.1. | ensimmäinen tammikuuta (spoken: eka tammikuuta) |
| 2.2. | toinen helmikuuta (spoken: toka helmikuuta) |
| 8.6. | kahdeksas kesäkuuta |
| 15.5. | viidestoista toukokuuta |
| 24.12. | kahdeskymmenesneljäs joulukuuta (Christmas) |
| 1.5. | ensimmäinen toukokuuta (Vappu / May Day) |

**Writing dates:** Finnish writes a period after the number: **1.1.** not 1/1.

### Question words for dates

- **Milloin?** — When?
- **Kuinka mones?** — What position / How manyeth?
- **Monesko?** — What date? (spoken alternative)

Example:
- *Milloin sun syntymäpäivä on?* — When is your birthday?
- *Mun syntymäpäivä on eka syyskuuta.* — My birthday is the 1st of September.

## SVO Sentence

### Sentence Types

**Sentence types** are templates for building sentences. Learning them makes creating correct Finnish sentences much easier.

- SVO sentence
- Predicative sentence
- Necessive structure
- (and others)

### Every Finnish sentence needs at minimum a VERB

- *Sataa* (It rains) — no subject needed
- *Mennään* (Let's go) — subject implied in the ending

### The SVO Sentence (Subject-Verb-Object)

**Subject + Verb + Object**

Example: *Lisa ostaa kirjan*
- Lisa = subject (who)
- ostaa = verb (buys)
- kirjan = object (book, in genitive)

### Transitive vs. Intransitive Verbs

**Transitive** (can have an object): ostaa, syödä, lukea, siivota, juoda, opiskella

**Intransitive** (cannot have an object): olla, asua, istua, seisoa, kävellä, juosta, tulla, mennä
- With these verbs, use **local cases** (missä/mihin/mistä) instead of objects

### The Object: Three Cases

#### Partitive (most common)
- Negative sentence: *En osta kirjaa*
- Indefinite quantity: *Otan kahvia* (some coffee)
- Action not finished: *Luen kirjaa* (still reading)
- Verbs that require partitive: opiskella, tahtoa, pelätä, tarvita…

#### Nominative
- Imperative: *Osta kirja!*
- Passive: *Ostetaan kirja*
- Necessive: *Minun täytyy ostaa kirja*

**BUT:** Partitive still wins if there's a reason, even in these forms.

#### Genitive
- Action completed/finished: *Luin kirjan* (I read the book — done)
- Countable whole item: *Ostan tietokoneen*

With personal pronouns, use the **accusative** form: *Minä tapaan sinut* (not sinun).

### Summary Table

| Case | When to use | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Partitive** | Negative, indefinite quantity, action ongoing | En osta kirjaa |
| **Nominative** | Command, passive, necessity | Osta kirja! |
| **Genitive** | Action finished, countable whole item | Luin kirjan |

**Partitive ALWAYS wins if there is a reason for it.**

### Elimination Rules (Decision Tree)

1. Negative sentence? → PARTITIVE
2. Indefinite/uncountable quantity? → PARTITIVE
3. Partitive verb (opiskella etc.)? → PARTITIVE
4. Imperative? → NOMINATIVE
5. Passive? → NOMINATIVE
6. Necessity (täytyy)? → NOMINATIVE
7. Default → GENITIVE

## Predicative Sentence

### Pattern: Subject + olla + Adjective/Description

The describing word is called the **predicative** (predikatiivi).

- *Kahvi on hyvää* (Coffee is good)
- *Hän on opiskelija* (He is a student)
- *Mun auto on uusi* (My car is new)

### Countable vs. Uncountable Words

**Countable (laskusana):** Concrete, indivisible things you can count. Break them and they stop being the same thing.
- Examples: pöytä, auto, ihminen

**Uncountable (ainessana):** Mass nouns, food, drinks, abstract things. Divide them and they're still the same substance.
- Examples: spagetti, vesi, kahvi, rakkaus

Some words can be both depending on context.

### The Adjective Case Rule

| Subject type | Adjective form | Example |
|-------------|----------------|---------|
| Uncountable | Partitive | *Kahvi on hyvää* |
| Countable | Nominative | *Auto on uusi* |
| Plural | Plural partitive | *Talot ovat isoja* |

**Negation does NOT change the case:**
- *Kahvi on hyvää* → *Kahvi ei ole hyvää* (both partitive)
- *Auto on uusi* → *Auto ei ole uusi* (both nominative)

### Question Words

| Subject | Question word | Example |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Uncountable | Millaista? | *Millaista kahvi on?* |
| Countable | Millainen? | *Millainen auto on?* |
| Plural | Millaisia? | *Millaisia talot ovat?* |

## Necessive Structure

### What is it?

Expresses **necessity** — what someone MUST do or HAS TO do.

### Formula

**Genitive subject + must verb + basic form of action verb**

Example: *Mun täytyy mennä*
- *Mun* = genitive (I / my)
- *täytyy* = must
- *mennä* = go (basic form — never conjugated!)

### Subject in Genitive

| Person | Genitive |
|--------|---------|
| I | mun |
| you | sun |
| he/she | hänen |
| we | meidän |
| you (plural) | teidän |
| they | heidän |

With names: *Liisan täytyy ostaa puhelin* (Lisa must buy a phone)

### Three Ways to Express Necessity

- **täytyy** — most common: *Mun täytyy ostaa kahvia*
- **pitää** — equally common: *Mun pitää ostaa kahvia*
- **on pakko** — also used: *Mun on pakko ostaa kahvia*

All three mean the same thing.

### Negative Form

In negative, use **ei tarvitse** (NOT "ei täytyy"):
- *Mun ei tarvitse mennä* (I don't have to go)
- *Sun ei tarvitse lähteä* (You don't have to leave)

Other negative options:
- *Mun ei ole pakko ostaa*
- *Mun ei tarvii ostaa* (spoken)

### Object Case in Necessive Sentences

- Usually **nominative**: *Liisan täytyy ostaa uusi puhelin*
- Partitive still wins if needed: *Mun on pakko opiskella suomea* (opiskella always takes partitive)

### Questions

Add **-ko/-kö** to the must verb:
- *Täytyykö sun mennä?* (Do you have to go?)
- *Onko sun pakko mennä?* (also common)

### Softer Obligation: kannattaa / on hyvä

- *Sun kannattaa lukea tää kirja* — You should read this book (it's worth it)
- *Sun on hyvä lukea tää kirja* — It's good for you to read this book

Less commanding than täytyy — more of a recommendation.

### Summary Table

| Element | What it is | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Subject | Genitive | mun, sun, hänen |
| Must verb | 3rd person, frozen | täytyy, pitää, on pakko |
| Action verb | Always basic form | mennä, ostaa, lukea |
| Object | Usually nominative | uusi puhelin |
| Negative | ei tarvitse | mun ei tarvitse mennä |

## Directions

### Vocabulary

| English | Finnish |
|---------|---------|
| street | katu |
| crossing / intersection | risteys |
| traffic lights | liikennevalot |
| on the left | vasemmalla |
| to the left | vasemmalle |
| on the right | oikealla |
| to the right | oikealle |
| forward / straight ahead | eteenpäin |
| straight | suoraan |
| past | ohi |
| close by | lähellä |
| far | kaukana |
| [distance] away | [distance] + päässä |

### Imperative for Directions

Take the **sä form** of the verb and remove the personal ending:

| Verb | Sä form | Imperative | Meaning |
|------|---------|------------|---------|
| mennä | sä menet | Mene! | Go! |
| kävellä | sä kävelet | Kävele! | Walk! |
| kääntyä | sä käännyt | Käänny! | Turn! |

**Negative imperative:** Add *älä* before the verb:
- *Älä mene!* — Don't go!
- *Älä kävele!* — Don't walk!
- *Älä käänny!* — Don't turn!

### Example Directions

*Olet tässä. Käänny pohjoiseen. Kävele eteenpäin. Käänny ensimmäisestä risteyksestä oikealle. Kävele eteenpäin. Käänny toisesta risteyksestä oikealle. Nyt, käänny oikealle. Olet perillä!*

Translation: You are here. Turn north. Walk forward. Turn right at the first crossing. Walk forward. Turn right at the second crossing. Now, turn right. You have arrived!

## KPT Change in Verb Types 3, 4, and 6

### Review: Regular KPT Change (Verb Type 1)

In Verb Type 1:
- **Strong consonant** in 3rd person: *nukkuu* (sleeps)
- **Weak consonant** in other forms: *en nuku* (don't sleep)

### Reverse KPT Change

In Types 3, 4, and 6 — the pattern is **reversed**:
- **Weak consonant** in the **basic form** (dictionary form)
- **Strong consonant** in **all personal forms**

### Example: Verb Type 3 — kuunnella (to listen)

| Form | Word | Pattern |
|------|------|---------|
| Basic form | kuunnella | weak (single n) |
| Mä kuuntelen | kuuntele- | strong (double nn) |
| Sä kuuntelet | kuuntele- | strong |
| Se kuuntelee | kuuntelee | strong |
| Me kuunnellaan (spoken) | kuunnella- | weak (from basic) |
| Te kuuntelette | kuuntele- | strong |

**Only -la/-lä ending verbs in Type 3 get reverse KPT.** Type 3 verbs ending in -na/-nä, -sta/-stä, -ra/-rä do NOT.

### Example: Verb Type 4 — tykkää (to like)

All personal forms use the **strong** form (kk):
- *Mä tykkään, sä tykkäät, se tykkää…*

### Tricky Type 4 Verbs — consonant appears from nowhere

| Basic form | Personal stem | Meaning |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| maata | makaa | to lie (down) |
| koota | kokoa | to gather |
| pelätä | pelkää | to be afraid |

These must be memorised — you cannot predict the strong form from the basic form.

### Verb Type 6 (eta/etä)

Also follows reverse KPT. Example: *pidetä* → personal forms use strong consonant.

### Summary

| Verb Type | Pattern |
|-----------|---------|
| Type 1 | Strong in 3rd person → weak elsewhere |
| Type 3 (-la/-lä only) | Weak in basic form → strong in personal forms |
| Type 4 (all) | Weak in basic form → strong in personal forms |
| Type 6 | Weak in basic form → strong in personal forms |

### Full Pronoun Table (Spoken Finnish forms)

| Case | mä | sä | hän/se | me | te | he/ne |
|------|----|----|--------|----|----|-------|
| Partitiivi | mua | sua | häntä/sitä | meitä | teitä | heitä/niitä |
| Genetiivi | mun | sun | hänen/sen | meidän | teidän | heidän/niiden |
| Inessiivi | mussa | sussa | hänessä/siinä | meissä | teissä | heissä/niissä |
| Elatiivi | musta | susta | hänestä/siitä | meistä | teistä | heistä/niistä |
| Illatiivi | muhun | suhun | häneen/siihen | meihin | teihin | heihin/niihin |
| Adessiivi | mulla | sulla | hänellä/sillä | meillä | teillä | heillä/niillä |
| Ablatiivi | multa | sulta | häneltä/siltä | meiltä | teiltä | heiltä/niiltä |

**Note:** In spoken Finnish, *se* and *ne* are used instead of *hän* and *he*. The accusative of *se* and *ne* does not add a -t ending.

### When to Use Each Case

| Case | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Partitiivi | With certain verbs (love, like etc.) | *Mä rakastan heitä* (I love them) |
| Genetiivi | Possession — someone's something | *Meidän talo on keltainen* (Our house is yellow) |
| Inessiivi | "In" — qualities a person has | *Hänessä on monta hyvää asiaa* (There are many good things about her) |
| Elatiivi | With verbs like tykkää (to like) | *He tykkää susta* (They like you) |
| Illatiivi | With verbs like rakastua (to fall in love) | *Ashok on rakastunut suhun* (Ashok is in love with you) |
| Adessiivi | "To have" structure | *Teillä on ihana koira* (You have a lovely dog) |
| Ablatiivi | From someone | *Mä sain tän lahjan heiltä* (I got this present from them) |
| Allatiivi | To / for someone | *Onko tää mulle?* (Is this for me?) |
| Akkusatiivi | Direct object | *Mä näin sut eilen* (I saw you yesterday) |

### Kenellä / Keneltä / Kenelle

| Question | Meaning | Pronoun | Name | Other noun |
|----------|---------|---------|------|-----------|
| Kenellä? | Who has? | mulla | Ashokilla | isoäidillä |
| Keneltä? | From whom? | multa | Ashokilta | isoäidiltä |
| Kenelle? | To/for whom? | mulle | Ashokille | isoäidille |

**Verbs taking -lle (to/for someone):** antaa, lähettää, sanoa, vastata, lainata (to lend)

**Verbs taking -lta/-ltä (from someone):** saada, ottaa, kysyä, kuulla, lainata (to borrow)

Example sentences:
- *Mulla on kirja.* — I have a book.
- *Sain kirjan Ashokilta.* — I got the book from Ashok.
- *Annan kirjan isoäidille.* — I give the book to grandma.

### Location Words — Täällä, Tuolla, Siellä

| Word | Missä? | Mistä? | Mihin? |
|------|--------|--------|--------|
| here (general) | täällä | täältä | tänne |
| over there (visible) | tuolla | tuolta | tuonne |
| there (not here) | siellä | sieltä | sinne |
| this (tämä) | tässä | tästä | tähän |
| that (tuo) | tuossa | tuosta | tuohon |
| it/that (se) | siinä | siitä | siihin |

### Täällä vs. Tässä

| Word | Meaning | Use when... |
|------|---------|------------|
| täällä | here (general area) | Talking about a broader location — a room, building, city |
| tässä | here (this exact spot) | Pointing to something very specific, in your hand, right here. Can be followed by a noun: *tässä laukussa* (in this bag) |

- *Olen täällä.* — I am here (somewhere in this area).
- *Tässä on se kirja.* — Here is the book (handing it over, right here).

## Grammar Rules at a Glance

### Predicative Adjective Cases
- Uncountable subject → adjective in **partitive** — *Kahvi on hyvää*
- Countable subject → adjective in **nominative** — *Auto on uusi*
- Negation does NOT change the case

### Opinion Expression
- **Musta / Susta / Siitä** = in [person]'s opinion (elative stem)
- **Mun / Sun / Hänen mielestä** = in [person]'s opinion (genitive)
- Both equally valid

### Transport
- Add **-lla/-llä** to any vehicle word: *bussilla, junalla, pyörällä*
- On foot = *kävellen* (different form, no -lla)

### Dates
- Ordinal number + month in partitive (-ta): *kahdeksas kesäkuuta*
- 1st = *ensimmäinen / eka*, 2nd = *toinen / toka*
